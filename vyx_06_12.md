# VYX (NCR Voyix) - Casual Summary

## TL;DR

The whole VYX thesis is basically:

> The market still thinks this is a dying legacy hardware company, but management is trying to turn it into a recurring software and payments business. If that transition works, the stock is cheap. If it doesn't, the stock deserves to be cheap.

At around $8/share, you're mostly betting on a turnaround.

---

## What does the company actually do?

NCR Voyix is the company behind a lot of the point-of-sale systems used by big restaurant chains, retailers, gas stations, convenience stores, etc.

Think:

- Cash registers
- Restaurant ordering systems
- Store checkout software
- Payment processing infrastructure

A lot of people hear "NCR" and think old-school hardware. The bull case is that they're becoming more of a software + payments company instead.

The analogy I'd use is:

> They don't really want to sell cash registers anymore. They want to be the software layer that runs the cash register and takes a cut of every transaction.

---

## Why is the stock interesting?

The market hates it because revenue looks ugly.

Reported revenue is actually declining because they intentionally stopped manufacturing a lot of their hardware themselves and outsourced it.

So on paper it looks like:

- Revenue down
- Old legacy company
- Tons of debt
- Not much growth

The bull case says those numbers are misleading.

Underneath the surface:

- Recurring revenue is growing
- Software ARR is growing
- EBITDA is improving
- Hardware is becoming a smaller part of the business

The argument is that Wall Street is looking at shrinking revenue and assuming the business is dying when it's actually restructuring.

---

## What's the moat?

The moat isn't that customers love NCR.

The moat is that replacing NCR is incredibly annoying.

If you're a huge restaurant chain running thousands of locations on NCR systems, switching means:

- Retraining employees
- Replacing terminals
- Migrating data
- Risking outages

So customers often stay simply because it's painful to leave.

The report describes this as a "sticky installed base."

---

## What's the biggest concern?

Free cash flow.

This is the key thing.

Management is showing:

- Improving EBITDA
- Improving margins

But actual free cash flow is still negative.

The company is guiding to a massive improvement in 2026.

Basically the story boils down to:

> Show me the cash.

If FCF turns strongly positive, the turnaround starts looking real.

If FCF doesn't show up, people will start worrying about debt and refinancing.

The report repeatedly comes back to this point.

---

## What about management?

The new CEO seems like a good fit.

He previously ran a payments company and has experience moving businesses toward recurring payments revenue.

The author likes the hire.

The issue is simply that he's only been there a few months, so nobody knows yet whether he can actually pull this off.

---

## Why might the stock go up?

If investors become convinced that:

1. The turnaround is working
2. Free cash flow is real
3. Software revenue keeps growing
4. Debt becomes less scary

Then the stock could rerate substantially.

The report argues that the market is pricing the company like failure is the most likely outcome.

If the company merely executes reasonably well, the stock could be worth materially more than $8.

---

## Why might the stock go down?

The bear case is pretty straightforward:

- Toast keeps stealing restaurant customers
- Software growth stays slow
- Free cash flow disappoints
- Debt becomes a problem
- Management fails to complete the transition

In that scenario, this isn't a turnaround story.

It's just a slowly shrinking legacy business.

---

## My Interpretation

This doesn't read like a "great company at a fair price" story.

It reads like a classic turnaround setup.

> The valuation is cheap because the market doesn't trust management's plan yet.

The stock is probably not going to work because revenue suddenly explodes.

It's going to work if:

- Margins improve
- Software becomes a larger percentage of the business
- Free cash flow finally starts matching the EBITDA

If I were following VYX, I'd spend way more time tracking:

- Quarterly FCF
- Software ARR growth
- Customer retention
- Debt leverage

And less time worrying about the headline revenue number, since that seems distorted by the hardware transition.
